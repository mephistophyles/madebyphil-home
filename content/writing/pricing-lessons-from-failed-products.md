I've launched four products over the past decade. Two failed primarily because of pricing mistakes. Here's what I learned.

## Mistake 1: Pricing Too Low

My first product was a productivity tool for freelancers. I priced it at $5/month because I wanted it to be "accessible." I thought low prices would drive volume.

What actually happened:

- Users didn't take it seriously at $5/month
- I couldn't afford any marketing
- Customer support requests cost more than the revenue they generated
- I couldn't justify investing in improvements

The product died slowly. Not from lack of users, but from lack of revenue to sustain development.

**The lesson:** Low prices aren't automatically better for customers. They signal low value and create unsustainable businesses.

## Mistake 2: No Free Tier

My second product was B2B software. I'd learned from the first mistake, so I priced it at $49/month. Professional pricing for a professional tool!

But I had no free tier, no trial, nothing. Users had to commit $49 before they could even see if the product worked for them.

Conversion rate: basically zero.

**The lesson:** People need to experience value before they pay for it. Especially for unknown products from unknown companies.

## Finding the Balance

The products that worked had:

1. **A genuine free tier** — Not a 14-day trial, but actual free usage with real limitations
2. **Clear upgrade triggers** — Users naturally hit limits that made upgrading obvious
3. **Prices that reflected value** — High enough to sustain the business, justified by the problem being solved

## The Pricing Framework That Works

After many failures, I settled on a simple framework:

1. **What's the alternative?** If your users would otherwise pay $500/month for a competing solution, you can charge $200 and still be a bargain.

2. **What's the pain worth?** If your product saves 10 hours per month and your users' time is worth $100/hour, you're creating $1,000 in value.

3. **Capture 10-20% of the value you create.** If you're saving someone $1,000/month, charging $100-200 is fair.

## Price Increases Are Easier Than You Think

I was terrified of raising prices on existing customers. What if they all leave?

Reality: When I raised prices 40% on one product, less than 5% of customers churned. Most didn't even notice. Those who did were generally understanding—they knew the product had improved.

**The lesson:** If everyone's happy with your prices, they're probably too low.

## The Meta-Lesson

Pricing isn't a math problem. It's a psychology problem. The "right" price isn't the one that maximizes some formula—it's the one that aligns with how customers perceive your value.

And you can only learn that by shipping, observing, and adjusting.
