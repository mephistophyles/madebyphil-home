There's a particular kind of freedom that comes from starting a project without knowing exactly where it will end up. Not recklessness—more like exploration.

## The Case Against Planning Everything

I used to be a meticulous planner. Every project started with detailed specs, diagrams, and timelines. And while that approach has its place, I've found that it often kills the most interesting ideas before they have a chance to develop.

The problem with detailed upfront planning is that it assumes you already know what you're building. But the best projects I've worked on evolved significantly from their original conception. The finished product looked nothing like what I imagined at the start—and that's a feature, not a bug.

## Starting with a Direction, Not a Destination

Now I prefer to start with a general direction rather than a specific destination:

- **"I want to make API development easier"** rather than "I'll build a mock server with these exact features"
- **"The living room needs more storage"** rather than "I'll build a 6-shelf unit with these dimensions"
- **"We need better customer communication"** rather than "We'll implement this specific workflow"

The direction gives you momentum. The specifics reveal themselves as you work.

## When This Doesn't Work

To be fair, this approach isn't universal. Some projects genuinely need detailed planning:

- Anything involving significant financial investment
- Projects with hard deadlines and fixed requirements
- Work that requires coordination across many people

But for personal projects, explorations, and early-stage ideas? Start building. You'll learn more in the first hour of work than in a week of planning.

## The Joy Part

There's genuine joy in discovering what you're building as you build it. Each decision opens new possibilities you couldn't have seen from the starting point. The project becomes a conversation between your intentions and the material reality of what you're creating.

That conversation is where the interesting stuff happens.
