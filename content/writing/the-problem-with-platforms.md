There is a fascinating reckoning happening in tech stocks. I think there are many things contributing to this. But for now I want to highlight a few points. First off, the stock market is just one dimension on a company's success. Various tech companies beat on all metrics (beat in this case is beat analyst expectations for EPS, revenue, and any secondary metrics like subscription growth) and still saw their stocks get hammered. The stock market rebalancing we're seeing now, where many software companies are trading at sub 10x P/E multiples may be where these stocks belong. More on that another day. Right now I want to focus on just one dimension that I think is contributing (and will continue to affect these larger companies) to their malaise, and that is the platform play.

I'll focus on Intercom, Atlassian, ServiceNow, and SalesForce. But this pattern holds in MANY other publicly listed and privately held SaaS companies. As a quick explanation of the platform play: none of these companies currently really sell their original product anymore. It has morphed into a platform that requires a significantly larger investment of resources to implement, integrate, and secondary products/modules/add-ons to truly function. This is excellent for enterprise customers who want to derive a ton more value from this platform, but it's a negative for anyone who just wants the product to address the core use case. 

- Atlassian's Jira server product has been end of lifed and the current cloud version is comparable but lacks the easy customization and in-depth access that its on prem cousins had (the remaining on-prem DC version now also in a terminal phase)
- Intercom's original product that lets you talk to customers across multiple channels is almost impossible to get without investing in a larger platform for all customer service needs. Fin, their customer service agent looks very impressive but requires a high degree of integration of product documentation, support knowledge bases, etc. 
- SalesForce is no longer the customizable CRM in the cloud. It's a mammoth system of record for enterprise salesforces. While it may have never been really simple, those looking for a customizable cloud-based CRM are likely checking all other options out first before they reluctantly consider SFDC.
- ServiceNow was once a light, fast ITSM product, but its NOW platform has stretched tendrils into things like product strategy, portfolio management, etc. and it markets itself as a platform as a service.

All these companies now focus on the enterprise customer segment at the expense of the mid market and SMBs. This is where challengers appear and eventually supplant incumbents (as these companies have done too). They price their products that way too (they have to, to cover all the investment and overhead), so smaller customers rarely see the ROI, especially when their free cash flow makes it hard to keep their smaller end customers happy.

A good example of the platform play being done right would be Google's suite of tools for business. While not their core business, it is a section that makes money, flywheels a lot of their other services, covers costs and allows them to offer things like Gmail for free to pretty much anyone.
