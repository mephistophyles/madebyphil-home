With the cost of building going down, at least in software, I think one of the key things for most companies to start to consider is building less. 

Hear me out. There are a number of things to consider and I think one of the elements that is really important is just because we can build more of the items in our backlog doesn't mean we should. We should maintain a different definition of cost to build and that is our customers' attention. 

The cost of building from an engineering perspective is only one factor to consider. Maintenance is another. The security nightmare and all the CI/CD challenges and PR reviews that need to happen for all this pent up work is a completely different story. It deserves its own consideration.

But I'm worried that a lot of software products, especially in the enterprise, with large teams that need to prove that they're shipping to keep their jobs are going to do just that. They won't have the political cover because the organization is demanding more velocity from their managers too. It's a short-sightedness that is going to lead them all to being replaced by smaller, leaner, and cheaper (both in pricing and maintenance) products that only cover the core value prop.

We should be building less in many cases because every bit of functionality needs a place. Either a menu or a button or something that runs in the background. If it is not serving your core value proposition, it is a potential distraction from it. 

The further you get from your top priority backlog items the less value it actually adds. The actual opportunity cost isn't in the form of "could we build something else instead?" that's one dimension. It's "if the users discover this functionality what are they not using?" What are they not exploring? Given that human attention is one of the limiting factors this should be considered. 

We're seeing people try and spend less time in each tool because they just want to get the job done and the tools are legitimately becoming faster and more efficient at their core job presently. Those are reasons to keep it around. 

I think one of the key metrics we need to get away from in most tools is daily or monthly active users or time in the platform. Maybe the right metric for some of these tools is just that they don't need to be open because they just work - it'd be workflows run without user intervention. If something needs to be constantly tweaked and adjusted and updated every week or every day because you added new conditions and considerations you are pushing a lot of the maintenance and overhead to your customers. And that's not value, it's the opposite of value.

Product managers were famously tasked with telling most of their stakeholders "no" most of the time, with engineering capacity being less of a constraint they need to understand the argument that it's too expensive to build is coming from the user. It's hard but they need to learn to say "no" more.
